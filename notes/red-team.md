# Red-team critique

## 1. Where this scenario is too conventional

It may overfit elite 2025-26 discourse: AI capex, data-center power, Taiwan gray-zone pressure, Ukraine frozen conflict, and U.S. polarization. The weirdest social outcomes may be understated: AI saints, synthetic dead relatives, religious schisms over machine confession, school refusal movements, or local anti-data-center populism becoming a national electoral bloc.

## 2. Where this scenario is overdetermined

The prompt asks for faster takeoff but no terminal calamity, which can nudge the model toward “muddle through with upheaval.” The baseline tries to resist that by including a U.S. 2030 correction, armed armistice, Taiwan crisis, major fraud escalation, and partial authoritarian stress. Still, the world may be either more stable because institutions slow everything, or more chaotic because concurrent shocks compound.

## 3. Where this scenario lacks mechanism

The weakest mechanisms are global South labor displacement, exact U.S. candidate emergence, and the timing of religious revival. These are included as causal tendencies rather than hard demographic predictions. The strongest mechanisms are infrastructure concentration, fraud/authentication response, school assessment redesign, and junior labor compression.

## 4. Where the writing is generic

Watch phrases such as “institutional lag,” “trust infrastructure,” and “machine labor.” They are useful but can become slogans. The essay needs concrete scenes: cloned-voice calls, oral exams, procurement fraud, pastoral AI policies, Taiwan quarantine insurance, data-center zoning fights.

## 5. Where social forces are weak

Religion and family risk appearing as reaction rather than driver. The rewrite integrates them as institutions that decide what counts as presence, verification, formation, confession, grief, authority, and human care.
